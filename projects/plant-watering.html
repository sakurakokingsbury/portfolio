Smart Watering System

Designed and built an automated indoor plant watering system that monitors environmental conditions, actuates a valve with a servo motor, and publishes sensor data to an IoT network over multiple days of deployment.

Product Design IoT Embedded Systems SolidWorks Arduino

Project Overview

This project focused on designing and manufacturing a compact automated watering system for indoor potted plants. The device was designed to fit a small pot, hold a water supply, monitor the plant environment, and automatically release water when soil conditions required it. The system also published sensor data to the Stevens IoT network for long-term monitoring. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

The final system measured four key parameters: soil moisture, atmospheric temperature, air humidity, and illuminance. It combined mechanical design, sensor integration, code development, and deployment testing in one product. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Mechanical Design

The final design was a compact 3D-printed attachment that clamps onto the side of a plant pot rather than replacing the pot itself. This approach saved space, kept the product visually sleek, and allowed it to support the water bottle, valve, and electronic components in one integrated structure.

After the first print, the model was revised because some components did not fit correctly and the initial version forgot to account for the water container. The updated design was adjusted to fit the pot more snugly and keep the electronics positioned in the back so they would stay away from water exposure.

Exploded view of the final watering system assembly.

Design Process

The project began with hand sketches from each team member, followed by evaluation of multiple concepts against the assignment requirements. The chosen design was selected because it best met the print time, size, and component-integration constraints. Alternative designs were rejected because they were either too large to print in time or did not provide enough space for all required hardware.

Electronics and System Architecture

  • Soil moisture sensor
  • DHT sensor for temperature and air humidity
  • Photocell for illuminance measurement
  • Servo motor for valve actuation
  • Microcontroller and IoT publishing setup

The system architecture centered on a microcontroller that processed input from the sensors and used a servo motor to open and close the valve when the soil moisture dropped below a threshold. The system then published readings to the course network on a repeating interval. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Final physical assembly mounted onto the plant pot.

Software and Control Logic

The software collected readings from the DHT sensor, photocell, and soil moisture sensor, converted raw measurements where needed, and published the data to MQTT topics on the Stevens IoT network. The system was configured to publish every 10 seconds. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Watering behavior was controlled through a soil-moisture threshold: when soil humidity fell below the chosen value, the servo opened the valve, held it open briefly, and then returned it to the closed position. The servo open and close angles were found experimentally through testing. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Challenges and Engineering Decisions

First print had fit issues

The initial print did not correctly fit all required parts and did not include enough room for the water container, so the design had to be revised and reprinted.

Valve and servo integration

The valve had to be partially disassembled and modified so the servo could actuate it without excessive strain.

Sensor code debugging

A soil humidity issue appeared only in the main sketch, even though the sensor worked in isolation, which required rebuilding the code structure during debugging.

IoT publishing failure during deployment

The deployed system initially collected data locally but failed to publish it online until the missing mqtt_loop(); line was identified and added.

These issues are described directly in the final report, especially the redesign of the print, the servo angle testing, and the missing MQTT loop that prevented successful data publishing until the final fix. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Design Performance

After deployment, the system successfully collected and published multi-day data showing expected trends in temperature, humidity, soil moisture, and illuminance. The report notes that illuminance varied more significantly than temperature, while moisture-related data changed in response to watering events and environmental conditions. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

The finished design met the project requirements by fitting the pot, monitoring all four variables, automatically controlling water flow, and publishing sensor data to the network. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}
